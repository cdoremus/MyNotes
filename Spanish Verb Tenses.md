# Gerunds (ing verbs)
- *gerundio en español* 
- Also known as *present participle*
## Forming the gerund from the verb

#### ar verbs - add *ando* to the verb base 
- hablando - speaking 
#### ir & er verbs - add *iendo* to the verb base
- comiendo - eating
- viviendo - living 

## Exceptions to gerund creation 
#### -ir verbs
Some irregular verbs ending in -ir, the -e in the verb stem becomes an -i
- diciendo (telling) for decir
- sintiendo (feeling) for sentir 
- pidiendo (ordering/asking) for pedir
#### -ir and -er verbs
Some verbs have the -o changed to -u
- durmiendo (sleeping) for dormir
If the verb stem ends in a vowel, then the ending would be -yendo
- leyendo (reading) for leer
- yendo (going) for ir
After -ll or -ñ, the -ibis dropped in -iendo
- riñendo (scolding) for reñir
- escabullendo (sneaking away) for escabullir 
#### Other exceptions 
- viendo (watching) for ver
- oyendo (listening) for escuchar

## When to avoid gerunds 
Gerunds are not used in Spanish as often as they are in English

- **Use infinitive after a preposition (e.g. *en, por, con, de, sobre, antes, después*) instead of a gerund.**
Thanks for being here
*Gracias por estar aqui* 

- **Don't use gerunds as nouns**
Learning is fun.
*Aprender es divertido.*

I like studying Spanish.
*Me gusta estudiar español* 

He talks about cooking every day 
*Él habla de cocinar cada los días.*

## Progressive tenses - using estar with a gerund

### Present progressive tense

**Use *estar* when pairing a gerund with a 'to be' form in the present (it's currently happening)**
- Estamos hablando ahora - We are talking now 
- Estoy comiendo fruta - I'm eating fruit 
- ¿Que estas haciendo? - What are you doing?

This is called the present progressive tense, in Spanish, the *presente progresivo* or the *presente continuo* tense (as opposed to the simple present tense of *estar*)

### Past and future progressive tenses

A gerund can be used with the past or future tense of *estar* too to describe something that was happening or will happen 

*Estaba hablando.*
I was eating 

*Estaré hablando.*
I will be speaking.

See https://youtu.be/CA8RdCFF_VI?si=dQV0N3amRSsQJCn7

### Key Progressive Tenses in Spanish:

**Present Progressive** *(El presente progresivo*)
Used for actions happening now or currently in progress. 
Structure: estar (present tense) + gerund
Example: Estoy estudiando (I am studying). 

**Imperfect Progressive** *(El imperfecto progresivo)*
Describes ongoing actions in the past that were interrupted or occurred simultaneously with another event.
Structure: estar (imperfect tense) + gerund
Example: Estaba cocinando cuando llegaste (I was cooking when you arrived). 

**Preterite Progressive** (*El pretérito progresivo*)
Used for actions that were ongoing in the past but have now finished.  This tense is less common and not directly equivalent in English.
Structure: estar (preterite tense) + gerund
Example: Estuvimos viendo la película (We were watching the movie). 

**Future Progressive** *(El futuro progresivo)*
Expresses actions that will be in progress at a specific time in the future. 
Structure: estar (future tense) + gerund
Example: Estarás trabajando cuando llegue (You will be working when I arrive). 

**Conditional Progressive** (El pcondicional progresivo)
Used to describe hypothetical ongoing actions in the past or future under certain conditions.
Structure: estar (conditional tense) + gerund
Example: Estaría leyendo si tuviera tiempo (I would be reading if I had time).

## Preterite (pretérito indefinido)

***The pretérito indefinido is used to talk about events in the past that have been completed. They often use the time specifier pasado or ayer. Those sentences can also use hace when it refers to 'ago'.***

*Ayer comí un melocotón.*
Yesterday I ate a peach.

*Nació en 1976.*
He was born in 1976.

*Estudiaste en Salamanca.*
You studied in Salamanca.

Most irregular verbs and some regular verbs have irregular endings in the preterite tense. Each of the listed imperfect verbs above notes both the present and preterite tense conjunctions.

####  Preterites with a/e->i stem change

Certain verbs have an irregular stem with the letter "i" in it. It is extremely important to remember that these verbs are not considered stem changers in the preterite tense although some of them may have been stem changers in the present tense. Just like the u‐stem verbs, i‐stem verbs do not follow the rules of a stem‐changing verb in the preterite and do not use the normal endings a stem‐changing verb uses. The verbs in the following list have a completely different stem that is used for every form of the preterite. Because they are irregular, they use the irregular endings

- [[#hacer - to make/to do/to ask]] - hic- (except 3rd person singular which is hiz-)
- [[#querer - to want]] - quis-
- [[#venir - to come]] - vin-

#### Preterites with J Stem change

- dij- for [[#decir - to say, to tell]]
- traj- for [[#traer - to bring]]
- conduj- for [[#conducir]]
- produj- for producir
- traduj- for traducir 
- 
The irregular verbs that end in the letter j use the same endings as the other irregular preterite verbs with one exception. In this case, the third person plural ending of the verb decir is –eron. Any time the irregular stem ends in j, the ellos, ellas, and ustedes endings will lose the letter i and become –jeron.

#### Preterites with e->ey stem change 
For verbs like leer and creer -yó replaces endings like -ió for él, ella, usted: Él leyó ayer. Ella no creyó. In the third person plural -yeron replaces -ieron: Ellos no leyeron nada.

#### Preterites with c->qu stem change  in the yo form
yo communiqué - I communicated (comunicar)
yo ubiqué - I located (ubicar)
yo busqué - I searched (buscar)

### Preterite Summary 
Use the preterite in the context of the past to: 
- say what someone did in the past 
- say that a new action suddenly occurred 
- say that actions occurred one after the other

## Imperfect tense (pretérito imperfecto)
pretérito imperfecto= imperfecto tense
pretérito indefinido = preterite tense

1. To describe things you ***always did* or you *use to do* in the past but can still be ongoing**. Often paired with something like *de niño* (as a child). For instance:
*De niño yo jugaba el fútbol* 
As a child, I use to play soccer

It can also be used by itself:

*Yo era un buen alumno*
 I was always a good student 

2. For things in progress in the past 

3. For talking about age

4. For talking about time and dates

5. Descriptions in the past

### Describing a scene

In contrast with the preterite indefinido, **the imperfecto is used for describing a scene. Therefore, the imperfecto gives background information about the situation.**

*Cuando apuntó al arco, estaba rodeada de jugadoras.*
When she aimed at the goal, she was surrounded by players.

### Simultaneous events 

***The imperfecto is also used to describe two things that were happening at the same time.***

*Mientras la delantera avanzaba, la defensa se compactaba.*
While the forward was advancing, the defense was closing in (lit. compacting).

Often when describing a scene, the continuous form of estar is used in the imperfecto with a present participle such as hablando.

*Estaba hablando con Ana cuando empezó el partido.*
I was speaking with Ana when the game began.

*Anoche estábamos viendo la tele cuando llegó Roberto.*
Last night we were watching TV when Roberto arrived.

While (*mientras*) and when *(cuando)* are often used in this situation.
### Summary:
The imperfect tense is translated by preceding the verb with "was" or "use to".
Both
I ***was*** working and
I ***use to*** work 
is translated to *Trabajaba* in the Spanish imperfect tense.

In the preterite you would not need any other words. I worked would just be *Trabajé*

### The pretérito imperfecto described in Babbel

***By using the pretérito imperfecto, you describe the characteristics of people or things.***

*Antes tenías el pelo muy largo.* 
You used to have very long hair.

*Recuerdo que tu bicicleta era roja.*
I remember that your bicycle was red.

***The imperfecto can also describe a condition or an overall situation in the past.***

*Mi padre ganaba mucho y vivíamos en una casa grande.*
My father earned a lot, and we used to live in a big house.

***It can also express repeated actions or routines in the past.***

*Tenía malas notas y pensaba siempre en chicas lindas.*
I had bad grades, and I used to always think about pretty girls.

### Summary use of the pretérito imperfecto

Use the pretérito imperfecto in the past to:
- describe what someone was doing in the past
- describe actions that were already in progress
- describe actions that occur at the same time

# Present Perfect (pretérito perfecto)

The pretérito perfecto, also known as the Spanish **present perfect tense**, is used to describe ***actions in the past that have a connection to the present moment***. This ***tense is formed by conjugating the verb "haber" in the present tense and adding the past participle of the main verb***. For example, "*He comido paella hoy*" (I have 


eaten paella today) illustrates an action that is part of recent experiences and still connected to the present. Another one is "*¿Qué has estado haciendo (hoy)?*" (What have you been doing (today))
### Formula 
***haber present tense conjugation + past participle*** 

### Haber present tense conjugación

| Person               | Conjugation | Translation     |
| -------------------- | ----------- | --------------- |
| yo                   | he          | I have...       |
| tú                   | has         | You have...     |
| él/ella/usted        | ha          | He has...       |
| nosotros(as)         | hemos       | We have...      |
| vosotros(as)         | habéis      | You all have... |
| ellos/ellas/ ustedes | han         | They have...    |

In Spain, the *pretérito perfecto* is commonly used to talk about ***recent past events even if they occured today***.

**Sentences that use *últimamente* (ultimately), *recientemente* (recently),  *ya* (already), *todavía* (still, yet), often *alguna vez* (ever), nunca (never) and siempre (always) will use present perfect verbs.**  

However, in many Latin American countries, the pretérito indefinido is more frequently used for such past events, regardless of their proximity to the present.

**Time indicators that use este/está, hoy and ya always require the *pretérito perfecto*. You will often use time markers like *esta mañana* (this morning) or *esta semana* (this week) that imply the recent past.**

Just like the present perfect in English, you use the *pretérito perfecto* even when you're speaking about actions that are still in progress.

### Regular past participle endings
For regular verbs, the past participle is formed by removing the infinitive ending (-ar, -er, -ir) and adding -ado, -ido, -ido, respectively. 
- ***-ar verbs have -ado endings*** 
- ***-er & -ir verbs have -ido endings***
For irregular verbs, the past participle uses a different form.

This tense usually expresses " I/you/we/they have..." like this:
- Yo: **he** comido - I have eaten 
- Tú: **has** comido - You have eaten 
- Él/Ella/Usted: **ha** comido - He/she have eaten 
 - Nosotros/Nosotras: **hemos** comido - We have eaten 
- Vosotros/Vosotras: **habéis** comido - You have eaten 
- Ellos/Ellas/Ustedes: **han** comido - They have eaten 

Note that "have been" is expressed using *estado* like *he estado* for "I have been " where *estado* is the past participle of *estar*.

### Past Participle Irregulars
- hacer - hecho
  - to do - done
- abrir - abrito
  - to open - opened
- ir - ido
  - to go - gone
- escribir - escrito
  - to write - written 
- poner - puesto 
  - to put - put
- romper - roto
  - to break - broken 
- volver - vuelto
  - to come back - come back
- ver - visto
  - to see - seen
- decir - dicho
  - to say - said

The Present Perfect is not frequently used in Latin America. Instead, the Preterite is used

# Past Perfect (pluscuamperfecto)

***The pluscuamperfecto or past perfect (aka pluperfect) expresses an event that is finished and which happened before another event in the past.***

**To form the pluscuamperfecto you need the auxiliary verb haber in the imperfect and the past participle of the verb describing the action.**

The regular past participle formed using these endings:
- -ar verbs->-ado
- -er or -ir verbs->-ido

Also note [[#Past Participle Irregulars]]

*Luis había lavado toda la vajilla.*
Luis had washed all the dishes.

*Cuando llegué, él ya se había ido.*
When I arrived, he had already left.

*Quería ayudarles, pero ellos ya habían limpiado todo.*
I wanted to help them, but they had already cleaned everything.

*He hecho las compras, pues no sabía que tú ya las habías hecho.*
I've done the shopping, since I didn't know that you had already done it.

*Quería ayudarle, pero ella ya había limpiado todo.*
I wanted to help her, but she had already cleaned everything.

*He hecho las compras, pues no sabía que tú ya las habías hecho.*

# Future tense 

## Simple future tense 
In Spanish, there are several ways to express the future (***futuro simple***) tense. The simple future tense is formed by adding specific endings to the infinitive form of the verb, which are:
 -é, -ás, -á, -emos, -éis, and -án, 
depending on the subject. For example, "hablaré" means "I will speak" 

**The Simple Future temse is often used in Spain as the future tense** 
### Simple future tense endings
***The futuro simple is formed by adding certain endings to the infinitive.***
yo -é
tú -ás
él/ella/usted -á
nosotros/nosotras -emos
vosotros/vosotras -éis
ellos/ellas/ustedes -án

*No trabajaremos en diciembre.*
We won't work in December.

*¿Comerás paella?*
Will you eat paella?

*Mañana dormiré hasta las 11.*
Tomorrow I will sleep until 11. 

*Ellos me llamarán pronto.*
You will call me soon.

*Hablaré contigo sobre eso. 
I will speak to you about that
### Irregular simple future forms

***For some verbs the infinitive is slightly changed to build the futuro simple***. For example, `hacer` and `decir` turn into har- and dir-

**hacer -> har-** (will do)
**decir -> dir-** (will say)

*El viernes hará sol.*
On Friday it will be sunny (lit. it will make sun).

*Nosotros no diremos nada.*
We won't say anything.

***With the verbs `tener`, `salir` and `venir` a dr is attached to the root.***
**tener -> tendr-**
**salir -> saldr-**
**venir -> vendr**

*Ella próximamente tendrá exámenes.*
She will have exams soon.

*Nosotros saldremos el viernes.*
We will go out on Friday.

*¿Ustedes vendrán pronto?*
Will you come soon?

***`Saber` and `poder` drop the e.***
**saber -> sabr-**
**poder -> podr-**

*¡Tú sabrás mucho!* 
You will know a lot!

*Ellos no podrán venir.* 
They will not be able to come.

You can also speak of the future using ir + a instead of the informative + ending. This is frequently used in Latin American. Here are two examples of both forms:

*Él va a ir de compras.*
*Él irá de compras.*
He will go shopping.

*¿Vais a tomar solo aqua?*
*¿Tomaréis solo aqua?*
Will you only drink water?

## Near future tense (ir + a + infinitive)
Another way to express the future is using the near future tense, which ***involves the construction "ir + a + infinitive form of the verb."*** This is often used to express actions that a be completed in the near future, such as:

*Voy a terminar mis deberes antes de la cena.*
I'm going to finish my homework before dinner

*Nosotras vamos a cenar en un restaurante.*
We are going to have dinner in a restaurant.

*Mi madre me va a llamar mañana.*
My mother is going to call me tomorrow.

*¿Tú no vas a venir con nosotros?*
Are you going to come with us?

*¿Vosotros vais a estudiar medicina?*
Are you going to study medicine?

*El fin de semana que viene voy a ir a la playa.*
Next weekend I'm going to go to the beach.

**~~The Near Perfect tense  is often used in Latin America as the future tense~~** 

## Common uses of simple and near future tenses

***Both the simple future and the near future are commonly used***, and their usage can vary depending on the context and the speaker's preference. Some English speakers learning Spanish might find the ir + a + infinitive form easier to use initially, but the simple future is also widely used and considered one of the easiest conjugation patterns to learn

# Conditional tense

## Real conditional verbs 

Also see [[Spanish Notes#Conditional sentences]]

In Spanish, ***you use the condicional (conditional)*** in everyday situations:

1. ***when you are asking for something politely***.

*¿Sería tan amable de ayudarme?*
Would you be so kind as to help me?

2. ***In addition, you use the conditional to express a wish.***

*Me gustaría ir al teatro esta noche.*
I would like (lit. It would please me) to go to the theater tonight.

3. ***The*** **condicional** ***is also used to express something you suppose.***

*Creo que te sería útil aprender español.*
I think it would be useful for you to learn Spanish.

4. It's common **in Spain** to use the condicional **to give advice**.

## Conditional conjugation 

***The endings for the conditional tense are the same as those for the -er and -ir forms of the imperfect tense, but they are attached to the infinitive*** rather than the stem. 

For example, the verb "viajar" (to travel) conjugates in the conditional as "viajaría" for "yo" (I), "viajarías" for "tú" (you), and "viajaría" for "usted, él, ella" (you, he, she).


### Regular conditional verbs 

***Regular verbs follow a standard set of endings:***
-ía for "yo" (I)
-ías for "tú" (you)
-ía for "usted, él, ella" (you, he, she)
-íamos for "nosotros" (we)
-íais for "vosotros" (you all)
-ían for "ustedes, ellos, ellas" (you all, they).

### Irregular conditional verbs 

**Irregular verbs** in the conditional tense use the same stems as in the future tense. Common irregular verbs include 
"decir" (to say) -> dir + conditional ending 
"hacer" (to do/make) -> habr + conditional ending
"venir" (to come) -> vendr + conditional ending
"tener" (to have) -> tendr + conditional ending
"poder" (to be able to) -> podr + conditional ending
"saber" (to know) -> sabr + conditional ending
"querer" (to want) -> querr + conditional ending
With some verbs, the condicional endings are added to a different base from the infinitive. These different bases correspond to the changed bases of irregular verbs in the future.

#### Irregular conditional patterns 

***With poder the e of the ending is dropped.***

*¿Podrías darme el folleto? (poder, tú)*
Could you give me the programme?

***With other verbs, the e is dropped from the infinitive but is replaced by a d.***

¿*Tendrías tiempo en el intermedio? (tener, tú)*
Would you have time in the intermission?

***There are other verbs which have a changed base (which is also the base for the future form). The condicional ending is added to this base.***

*Ellos no harían una obra para niños. (hacer)*
They wouldn't do a play for children.

*No querríamos trabajar por la noche. (querer)*
We wouldn't want to work nights.

*¿Qué diría el director? (decir)*
What would the director say?

## Gustar
The verb gustar which normally means **to like** in the conditional tense it means ***would like***. The phrase "me gustaría" (I would like) is commonly used to express what you would like to do in the future or to make polite requests. 

## Deber
The verb "deber" has a wholly different meaning in the conditional tense. It normally means ***to owe***, but in the conditional tense it means ***must or should.***


In addition, ***the conditional is used in reported speech to express future events from a past perspective,***  such as "*Dijo que vendría a la fiesta*" (He said he would come to the party).

The ***conditional tense can also be used to express hypothetical situations with "si" (if) clauses***. For example, "Si tuviera mucho dinero, iría de vacaciones" (If I had a lot of money, I would go on holiday).

In summary, the conditional tense in Spanish is a versatile tool for expressing hypothetical situations, probabilities, and polite requests, and it is formed using specific endings attached to the infinitive form of the verb.

## Conditional Perfect 

***By using the condicional perfecto (conditional perfect), you express what could have happened in the past.***

*Habría sido muy feliz con Paula.*
I would have been very happy with Paula.

***It consists of the forms of haber in the condicional (conditional) and the past participle of the main verb.***

*¿Te habrías casado con ella?* 
Would you have married her?

*Seguramente habríais vivido en el campo.*
Surely, you would have lived in the country.

***The past participle is formed with -ido for -ir and -er verbs. For -ar verbs, the ending is -ado.***

The conditional perfect is often translated as "would have" follow by the translated verb.

# Subjunctive Mood

The Spanish subjunctive is a mood, rather than a tense. It’s used in certain circumstances, such as:
- To express doubt
- To show feeling or emotion about something
- To show whether we think something is true or not

**Doubt, disbelief and denial are common subjunctive triggers**, but not the only ones.

We have the subjunctive in English too! Although it’s not as commonly used as the Spanish subjunctive, phrases such as the following use the English subjunctive:
“If I were you”
“So be it”
“If I had known”


### Present Subjective
***subjuntivo presente*** 

There are some situations in Spanish when you have to use the subjuntivo (subjunctive). This verb form occurs in different tenses in Spanish after que. There is no exact equivalent to subjunctive verb conjugation in English.

*¿Quieres que te ayude con los preparativos?*
Do you want me to (lit. that I) help you with the preparations?

*Espero que beban cerveza.*
I hope that they drink beer.

***You use the subjuntivo presente to express a wish.***

*Quiero que me ayudes con la fiesta.*
I want you to (lit. that you) help me with the party.

In this sentence, the first verb (querer) expresses a wish, followed by a second verb (ayudes) which is in the subjuntivo presente after que.

***The subjuntivo presente is also used to express a hope.***

*Espero que todos beban cerveza.*
I hope that everyone drinks beer.

The first verb (esperar) expresses the hope, followed by a second verb (beban) in the subjuntivo after que.

***You also use the subjuntivo presente after impersonal expressions***, such as Es... que. For example, Es mejor que... (It's better if/that...)

*Es mejor que compre su propia bebida.*
It's better if he/she buys his/her own drink.

### Sentence structure for the Subjunctive 

Use if the subjunctive in a sentence often has two verbs of this form:

\[indicative verb] + que \[verb in subjunctive]

*Dile a ella que lleve una chaqueta.* 
Tell her to wear her jacket.

*Te doy mi chaqueta para que no tengas frío.* 
I'm giving you my coat so you won't be cold.

*Busco un carro barato que funcione.* 
I'm looking for a cheap car that works

### Subjunctive with cuando 

**Use the subjunctive after cuando to talk about future events.**

Cuando yo viaje a España, visitaré Madrid.
When I travel to Spain, I'll visit Madrid.

*Llámame cuando recibas mi mensaje.*
Call me when you receive my message.

If we're talking about the future "cuando" takes the subjective. If you use the indicative, it sounds like a regular occurrence.
- *cuando me visitas* (all the time, regularly) 
- *cuando me visites* (next time, at some point in the future) = subjunctive 

### *Antes de que* subjunctive trigger

#### Pattern: antes de que + subjunctive verb

**In this pattern there are two clauses each having their own subject**. The subjunctive clause can be at the beginning or the end.

*Antes de que empiece la película, necesito comprar palomitas de maíz.*
Before the movie begins,  I need to buy popcorn

*Antes de que oscurezca, quiero encontrar una linterna.*
Before it gets dark, I want to find a flashlight

*Antes de que se enfríe el café, deberíamos beberlo.*
Before the coffee gets cold, we should drink it.

#### Alternative pattern: antes de + infinitive verb
This pattern is when there is only one subject in the sentence

*Suelo leer antes de acostarme.*
I usually read before going to bed.
 
#### Common words used in the subjunctive with que (triggers of desire, hope, influence, command or request)
- querer - to want
- esperar - to hope for
- desear - to desire
- preferir - to prefer
- recomendar - to recommend 
- insistir en - to insist on

### *para que* + subjunctive trigger

**Translates as "so" to express purpose or intent**  Used when there are two clauses and they have different subjects.

*Hago videos como este para que puedas mejorar tu español.*
I make videos like this one so you can improve your Spanish. 

*Compro ropa de buena calidad para que dure más tiempo.*
I buy good quality clothing so it lasts longer.

#### Alternative pattern: *para* + infinitive verb
Used when there is no subject change between clauses.

*Me levanto temprano para evitar el tráfico.*
I get up early so I can avoid the traffic.
